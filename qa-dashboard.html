Key Takeaways

1. Version B is the sweet spot

Perfect Tier 1 compliance across all 5 lessons. The skill performs best with moderate guidance. Why: With one verbatim DQ and one verbatim TT, there's exactly one slot for each — no orphaning problem. The looser outline gives the skill enough room to make good choices without the risk of missing a specific instruction.

2. Version A drops verbatim TT questions

In 4/5 lessons, one of two verbatim Target Task questions was completely dropped. Why: The skill's Claim+QuickWrite assessment pattern only has one natural slot for a verbatim TT. When two are provided, the second has nowhere to go. The one success (AM G4 L2) happened because TT #1 fit naturally as a Claim stem. See failure analysis above for the fix.

3. Version C mostly works

4/5 Version C drafts were solid. Why the one failure: WW G5 L18's text has two parallel characters (Delphine and Calinda) that Tilly describes very differently. Without a DQ count constraint, the skill generated separate question threads for each character plus a comparison question, hitting 7 DQs with Q2 and Q7 being near-duplicates.

4. Question overlap is a pattern

Flagged in 4/15 drafts. Why: The skill's question-generation logic adds a synthesis/capstone TDQ near the end of the sequence to tie earlier questions together. This is a sound pedagogical instinct, but the skill doesn't check whether the synthesis question substantially overlaps with an earlier narrower question. It's particularly common on texts with a single dominant theme (like arete) where every question circles back to the same concept.

5. No issues with length, grade level, or MC+HL limits

All 15 drafts passed on question length, accessible language, grade-level appropriateness, and the 2 MC+HL cap.
